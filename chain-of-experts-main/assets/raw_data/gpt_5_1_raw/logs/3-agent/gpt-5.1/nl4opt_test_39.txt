[MODELER]:
Here is a linear optimization model and its solution.

---

## 1. Define decision variables

Let:

- \(x\) = gallons of chocolate ice cream produced in a week  
- \(y\) = gallons of vanilla ice cream produced in a week  

Both are continuous and nonnegative:

\[
x \ge 0,\quad y \ge 0
\]

---

## 2. Objective function (maximize profit)

Profit per gallon:

- Chocolate: \$200 per gallon → \(200x\)  
- Vanilla: \$300 per gallon → \(300y\)

Maximize total weekly profit:

\[
\max \; Z = 200x + 300y
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

### 3.1 Production limits (minimum and maximum)

- At least 5 gallons and at most 10 gallons of chocolate:

\[
5 \le x \le 10
\]

- At least 5 gallons and at most 8 gallons of vanilla:

\[
5 \le y \le 8
\]

Equivalently in standard LP form:

\[
x \ge 5,\quad x \le 10,\quad y \ge 5,\quad y \le 8
\]

---

### 3.2 Time (hour) capacity

- Chocolate: 1 hour per gallon → total time \(= 1x\)
- Vanilla: 2 hours per gallon → total time \(= 2y\)

Available production time per week: 30 hours:

\[
1x + 2y \le 30
\]

---

### 3.3 Worker requirement

The text here is ambiguous in typical LP terms. A natural interpretation consistent with linear modeling is:

> At any time (i.e., per unit of production) chocolate requires 1 worker, vanilla requires 2 workers, and at least 6 workers must be available to cover those needs.

Using that interpretation, a per-gallon “worker requirement” would be:

- Chocolate: 1 worker per unit → contributes \(1x\)
- Vanilla: 2 workers per unit → contributes \(2y\)

At least 6 workers must be used:

\[
x + 2y \ge 6
\]

Given the other bounds (\(x \ge 5, y \ge 5\)), this constraint is automatically satisfied and is not binding, but formally it is:

\[
x + 2y \ge 6
\]

---

## 4. Complete mathematical model

\[
\begin{aligned}
\max\quad & Z = 200x + 300y \\
\text{s.t.}\quad 
& 5 \le x \le 10 \\
& 5 \le y \le 8 \\
& x + 2y \le 30 \\
& x + 2y \ge 6 \\
& x, y \ge 0
\end{aligned}
\]

(The last two nonnegativity constraints are redundant because of \(x,y \ge 5\), but are standard.)

---

## 5. Solve analytically

The constraints:

1. \(x \in [5, 10]\)  
2. \(y \in [5, 8]\)  
3. \(x + 2y \le 30\)

Check corner points of the feasible region:

- (5, 5):  
  - Time: \(5 + 2\cdot 5 = 15 \le 30\) (feasible)  
  - Profit: \(200\cdot 5 + 300\cdot 5 = 1000 + 1500 = 2500\)

- (10, 5):  
  - Time: \(10 + 2\cdot 5 = 20 \le 30\) (feasible)  
  - Profit: \(200\cdot 10 + 300\cdot 5 = 2000 + 1500 = 3500\)

- (5, 8):  
  - Time: \(5 + 2\cdot 8 = 21 \le 30\) (feasible)  
  - Profit: \(200\cdot 5 + 300\cdot 8 = 1000 + 2400 = 3400\)

- (10, 8):  
  - Time: \(10 + 2\cdot 8 = 26 \le 30\) (feasible)  
  - Profit: \(200\cdot 10 + 300\cdot 8 = 2000 + 2400 = 4400\)

The time constraint \(x + 2y \le 30\) does not bind at (10, 8), so it is still feasible.

Among all these feasible corner points, profit is maximized at:

- \(x = 10\) (chocolate gallons)  
- \(y = 8\) (vanilla gallons)  
- Maximum profit \(Z = \$4{,}400\)

Thus, to maximize profit:

- Produce 10 gallons of chocolate ice cream.  
- Produce 8 gallons of vanilla ice cream.

---

## 6. Suitable Python libraries

This is a small linear program; any LP-capable library will work:

- `PuLP` – very easy syntax for defining LPs, works with CBC or other solvers.
- `scipy.optimize.linprog` – basic LP solver in SciPy.
- `ortools.linear_solver` (Google OR-Tools) – industrial-strength solver, good for scaling and more complex models.

### Example using PuLP

```python
import pulp as pl

# Define problem
prob = pl.LpProblem("IceCreamProduction", pl.LpMaximize)

# Decision variables
x = pl.LpVariable("chocolate_gallons", lowBound=0)
y = pl.LpVariable("vanilla_gallons", lowBound=0)

# Objective
prob += 200 * x + 300 * y

# Constraints
prob += x >= 5
prob += x <= 10
prob += y >= 5
prob += y <= 8
prob += x + 2 * y <= 30
prob += x + 2 * y >= 6  # worker requirement as modeled above

# Solve
prob.solve()

print("Status:", pl.LpStatus[prob.status])
print("Optimal x (chocolate):", x.value())
print("Optimal y (vanilla):", y.value())
print("Max profit:", pl.value(prob.objective))
```

This will return approximately:

- `x = 10.0`
- `y = 8.0`
- `Max profit = 4400.0`

[CODER]:
import pulp as pl

def main():
    # Define problem
    prob = pl.LpProblem("IceCreamProduction", pl.LpMaximize)

    # Decision variables
    x = pl.LpVariable("chocolate_gallons", lowBound=0)
    y = pl.LpVariable("vanilla_gallons", lowBound=0)

    # Objective function: Maximize 200x + 300y
    prob += 200 * x + 300 * y

    # Constraints
    prob += x >= 5          # minimum chocolate
    prob += x <= 10         # maximum chocolate
    prob += y >= 5          # minimum vanilla
    prob += y <= 8          # maximum vanilla
    prob += x + 2 * y <= 30 # time capacity
    prob += x + 2 * y >= 6  # worker requirement

    # Solve the problem
    prob.solve()

    # Get the objective value
    obj_value = pl.value(prob.objective)

    # Final required output
    print(f"FINAL_OBJ: {obj_value}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
