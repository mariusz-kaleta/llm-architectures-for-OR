Here is a clean mathematical model of the problem and how you’d solve it.

---

## 1. Decision Variables

Let  
- \( x \) = number of small bins  
- \( y \) = number of large bins  

Both must be nonnegative integers:
- \( x \ge 0,\; y \ge 0 \)
- \( x, y \in \mathbb{Z} \)

---

## 2. Objective Function

A small bin holds 25 units; a large bin holds 60 units. We want to maximize the total amount of recycling collected:

\[
\text{Maximize } Z = 25x + 60y
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

1. **Worker availability**

   - Each small bin uses 2 workers  
   - Each large bin uses 5 workers  
   - At most 100 workers available  

   \[
   2x + 5y \le 100
   \]

2. **Relationship between numbers of bins**

   The number of small bins must be three times the number of large bins:

   \[
   x = 3y
   \]

3. **Minimum numbers of bins**

   - At least 10 small bins:

     \[
     x \ge 10
     \]

   - At least 4 large bins:

     \[
     y \ge 4
     \]

4. **Integrality**

   \[
   x, y \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}
   \]

---

## 4. Simplified Model Using the Relationship \(x = 3y\)

Substitute \( x = 3y \) into the constraints:

- Worker constraint:

  \[
  2(3y) + 5y \le 100 \Rightarrow 6y + 5y \le 100 \Rightarrow 11y \le 100
  \Rightarrow y \le \frac{100}{11} \approx 9.09
  \]

  Since \( y \) must be integer: \( y \le 9 \).

- Minimum bins:

  - From \( x \ge 10 \Rightarrow 3y \ge 10 \Rightarrow y \ge \frac{10}{3} \Rightarrow y \ge 4 \) (with integrality).
  - Also we already have \( y \ge 4 \) directly.

So:

\[
4 \le y \le 9,\quad y \in \mathbb{Z},\quad x = 3y.
\]

The objective becomes:

\[
Z = 25x + 60y = 25(3y) + 60y = 75y + 60y = 135y.
\]

Maximize \( 135y \) over integer \( y \in [4,9] \). This is clearly maximized at the largest allowed \( y \), i.e., \( y = 9 \).

Then:

- \( y = 9 \)
- \( x = 3y = 27 \)

Check workers:

\[
2x + 5y = 2(27) + 5(9) = 54 + 45 = 99 \le 100 \quad \text{(feasible)}
\]

Total recycling:

\[
Z = 25(27) + 60(9) = 675 + 540 = 1215 \text{ units}
\]

---

## 5. Final Answer

- Small bins: \( x = 27 \)  
- Large bins: \( y = 9 \)  
This combination respects all constraints and maximizes the total recycling collected (1215 units).

### Example Using PuLP

```python
import pulp as pl

# Problem
prob = pl.LpProblem("RecyclingBins", pl.LpMaximize)

# Decision variables (integer)
x = pl.LpVariable('small_bins', lowBound=0, cat='Integer')
y = pl.LpVariable('large_bins', lowBound=0, cat='Integer')

# Objective
prob += 25*x + 60*y

# Constraints
prob += 2*x + 5*y <= 100           # worker limit
prob += x == 3*y                   # small bins = 3 * large bins
prob += x >= 10                    # at least 10 small
prob += y >= 4                     # at least 4 large

# Solve
prob.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

print("Status:", pl.LpStatus[prob.status])
print("Small bins:", x.value())
print("Large bins:", y.value())
print("Total recycling:", 25*x.value() + 60*y.value())
```

This will output 27 small bins, 9 large bins, and 1215 units of recycling.

import pulp as pl

def main():
    # Define the problem
    prob = pl.LpProblem("RecyclingBins", pl.LpMaximize)

    # Decision variables
    x = pl.LpVariable('small_bins', lowBound=0, cat='Integer')
    y = pl.LpVariable('large_bins', lowBound=0, cat='Integer')

    # Objective function: Maximize total recycling
    prob += 25 * x + 60 * y

    # Constraints
    prob += 2 * x + 5 * y <= 100      # Worker availability
    prob += x == 3 * y                # Relationship between bins
    prob += x >= 10                   # Minimum small bins
    prob += y >= 4                    # Minimum large bins

    # Solve the problem
    prob.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

    # Compute final objective value
    final_obj = pl.value(prob.objective)

    # Required final print
    print(f"FINAL_OBJ: {final_obj}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
