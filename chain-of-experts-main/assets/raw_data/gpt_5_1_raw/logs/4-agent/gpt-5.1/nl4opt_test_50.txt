1. **Key optimisation goal**

   - Goal: Maximise total profit from producing and selling the two snack mixes, given limited stocks of cat paw snacks and gold shark snacks.

2. **Suggested model type**

   - Model: Linear Programming (LP) – a small, continuous, two‑variable LP.

3. **Main decision variables**

   Define decision variables as kilograms of each mix to produce:

   - Variable 1: Amount (kg) of Mix 1 to prepare.
   - Variable 2: Amount (kg) of Mix 2 to prepare.

4. **Objective function structure**

   - Each kg of Mix 1 yields $12 profit.
   - Each kg of Mix 2 yields $15 profit.
   - Objective: Maximise total profit = 12 × (kg of Mix 1) + 15 × (kg of Mix 2).

5. **Key constraints to consider**

   a. **Cat paw snack availability**

   - Mix 1 is 20% cat paw snacks; Mix 2 is 35% cat paw snacks.
   - Total cat paw used in both mixes ≤ 20 kg available.

   Conceptually:
   - 0.20 × (kg of Mix 1) + 0.35 × (kg of Mix 2) ≤ 20.

   b. **Gold shark snack availability**

   - Mix 1 is 80% gold shark snacks; Mix 2 is 65% gold shark snacks.
   - Total gold shark used in both mixes ≤ 50 kg available.

   Conceptually:
   - 0.80 × (kg of Mix 1) + 0.65 × (kg of Mix 2) ≤ 50.

   c. **Non‑negativity**

   - Both mix quantities must be ≥ 0; cannot produce negative amounts.

6. **Modelling/solution strategy**

   - Use an LP solver or graphical method:
     - Plot the two resource constraints in the (Mix 1, Mix 2) plane.
     - Identify the feasible region defined by:
       - Cat paw limit,
       - Gold shark limit,
       - Non‑negativity.
     - Evaluate the objective function at each corner (extreme) point of the feasible region.
   - Select the corner point that yields the highest profit; that gives the optimal kg of Mix 1 and Mix 2.

---

## 1. Decision variables

Let

- \(x_1 \ge 0\): kilograms of Mix 1 to produce  
- \(x_2 \ge 0\): kilograms of Mix 2 to produce  

---

## 2. Objective function

Profit per kg:

- Mix 1: \$12/kg  
- Mix 2: \$15/kg  

Maximise total profit:

\[
\max Z = 12x_1 + 15x_2
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

### a) Cat paw snack availability

Fractions of cat paw in each mix:

- Mix 1: 20% = 0.20  
- Mix 2: 35% = 0.35  

Total cat paw used cannot exceed the 20 kg available:

\[
0.20x_1 + 0.35x_2 \le 20
\]

### b) Gold shark snack availability

Fractions of gold shark in each mix:

- Mix 1: 80% = 0.80  
- Mix 2: 65% = 0.65  

Total gold shark used cannot exceed the 50 kg available:

\[
0.80x_1 + 0.65x_2 \le 50
\]

### c) Non‑negativity

\[
x_1 \ge 0,\quad x_2 \ge 0
\]

---

## 4. Full LP model

\[
\begin{aligned}
\max \quad & Z = 12x_1 + 15x_2 \\
\text{s.t.} \quad
& 0.20x_1 + 0.35x_2 \le 20 \quad \text{(cat paw)} \\
& 0.80x_1 + 0.65x_2 \le 50 \quad \text{(gold shark)} \\
& x_1 \ge 0,\ x_2 \ge 0
\end{aligned}
\]

---

## 5. Suitable Python libraries

For solving this linear program, good choices in Python are:

- **PuLP**: very simple LP modeling; works with CBC by default.
- **SciPy (`scipy.optimize.linprog`)**: good for small/medium LPs.
- **OR-Tools (Google OR-Tools Linear Solver)**: powerful and efficient for LP/MIP.

For a problem of this size, **PuLP** or **SciPy** are the most straightforward.

[CODER]:
import pulp

def main():
    # Create the LP problem: maximization
    model = pulp.LpProblem("Snack_Mix_Optimization", pulp.LpMaximize)

    # Decision variables
    x1 = pulp.LpVariable('x1', lowBound=0)  # kg of Mix 1
    x2 = pulp.LpVariable('x2', lowBound=0)  # kg of Mix 2

    # Objective function: Maximize profit
    model += 12 * x1 + 15 * x2

    # Constraints
    # Cat paw snack availability
    model += 0.20 * x1 + 0.35 * x2 <= 20

    # Gold shark snack availability
    model += 0.80 * x1 + 0.65 * x2 <= 50

    # Solve the problem
    model.solve(pulp.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

    # Get the objective value
    final_obj = pulp.value(model.objective)

    # Print final objective value in required format
    print(f"FINAL_OBJ: {final_obj}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
