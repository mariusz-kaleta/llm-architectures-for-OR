1. **Key optimisation goal**

   - Goal: **Minimise the total time for the mixture to become effective**, given that one ingredient must be added first and the mixture becomes effective only after *both* ingredients have completed their individual effectiveness times.
   - Interpretation: If sulfate is added first and ginger second, the total time is:
     - time until sulfate is effective (0.5 per unit, from its start time) and
     - time until ginger is effective (0.75 per unit, from its start time)
     - subject to ginger being added after sulfate (or vice versa).
   - The Modeller should clarify/choose a convention, but a common approach:
     - The **effective time of the mixture** is the time until the *later* ingredient finishes its required effectiveness, given they are added sequentially.

2. **Suggested model type**

   - Model: **Linear Programming (LP)** if we ignore integer restrictions (units as continuous quantities).
   - If the “one must be added before the other” is interpreted in a way that affects only timing, not the relationship between quantities, the core structure remains LP.
   - Optionally, a small **case-wise LP analysis**: solve assuming one order (sulfate first) and then the other (ginger first), and pick the better outcome.

3. **Main decision variables**

   Let:
   - \( S \): number of units of sulfate in the shampoo.
   - \( G \): number of units of ginger in the shampoo.

   For sequencing/timing, you can:
   - Interpret the objective purely in terms of **time per unit mixture**, which simplifies to minimising a linear function \(0.5S + 0.75G\) if you assume the timing effect is additive/weighted.
   - Or, if modelling strict sequence and completion time:
     - Assume ingredient A is added from time 0, ingredient B is added immediately after A finishes its own “activation process”, and the total effective time is driven by the schedule.
     - However, since the problem statement is ambiguous but usually treated in textbooks as a standard LP, the cleaner interpretation is:
       - Objective: minimise **total effectiveness time of ingredients** = \(0.5S + 0.75G\).

4. **Key constraints to consider**

   Core quantity constraints:
   1. **Minimum sulfate content**
      - Shampoo must contain **at least 100 units of sulfate**:
        - \( S \ge 100 \).

   2. **Total ingredient content**
      - The shampoo must have a **total of 400 units** of sulfate + ginger:
        - \( S + G = 400 \).

   3. **Sulfate not too large relative to ginger**
      - There can be **at most twice as much sulfate as ginger**:
        - \( S \le 2G \).

   4. **Non-negativity**
      - \( S \ge 0, \; G \ge 0 \).

   Optional/order-related interpretation (if modeller wants to reflect “one must be added before the other” more explicitly):
   - Treat the “note” as **not affecting feasibility**, only the conceptual story of the objective. Common exam-style solutions ignore separate start times and simply minimise the weighted sum of times.
   - If you choose to explicitly model order:
     - Create two *scenarios*:
       1. Sulfate added first, then ginger.
       2. Ginger added first, then sulfate.
     - In each scenario, define total effective time as the time at which both ingredients have completed their required per-unit effectiveness from their respective start times. This will require additional time-related variables and sequencing relations, and may no longer be purely linear unless strong simplifying assumptions are adopted.
     - Solve both scenarios as LPs (if linear form is maintained) and pick the one with minimum total time.

5. **Objective function structure**

   - Under the standard, simplified interpretation (recommended for the Modeller):
     - Objective: **minimise total effective time**:
       - Minimise \( 0.5S + 0.75G \)
     - Subject to:
       - \( S + G = 400 \)
       - \( S \ge 100 \)
       - \( S \le 2G \)
       - \( S, G \ge 0 \)

   This keeps the model linear and directly solvable.

[MODELER]:
Here is a clean mathematical model of the optimisation problem, plus notes on how to encode and solve it in Python.

---

## 1. Decision variables

Let

- \(S \ge 0\): number of units of sulfate in the shampoo  
- \(G \ge 0\): number of units of ginger in the shampoo  

(We treat them as continuous variables; if they must be integers, this becomes a small integer program.)

---

## 2. Constraints

1. **Total quantity requirement**

   The shampoo must have a total of 400 units:

   \[
   S + G = 400
   \]

2. **Minimum sulfate**

   At least 100 units of sulfate:

   \[
   S \ge 100
   \]

3. **Sulfate not too large relative to ginger**

   There can be at most twice as much sulfate as ginger:

   \[
   S \le 2G
   \]

   You can equivalently write this as \(S - 2G \le 0\).

4. **Nonnegativity**

   \[
   S \ge 0,\quad G \ge 0
   \]

---

## 3. Objective function

Two interpretations are possible; the standard LP interpretation (used in many textbooks and exams) is:

- Each unit of sulfate contributes 0.5 minutes of “effectiveness time”.
- Each unit of ginger contributes 0.75 minutes of “effectiveness time”.
- We minimise the **total effectiveness time across all units**:

\[
\min\; Z = 0.5 S + 0.75 G.
\]

This ignores the sequencing note in terms of strict scheduling and treats the times as additive contributions. The sequencing (“one must be added before the other”) is then just narrative and does not affect feasibility.

So the linear program is:

\[
\begin{aligned}
\min_{S,G}\quad & 0.5 S + 0.75 G \\
\text{s.t.}\quad
& S + G = 400 \\
& S \ge 100 \\
& S \le 2G \\
& S, G \ge 0.
\end{aligned}
\]

This is a standard linear programming problem.

---

## 4. (Optional) More literal “sequence/time to effectiveness” interpretation

If you try to model **completion time** explicitly, you need:

- A start time for the first ingredient;
- A start time for the second;
- A completion time variable that is the **maximum** of the two completion times.

Because the completion time becomes \(\max(\text{end\_time\_sulfate}, \text{end\_time\_ginger})\), that introduces a non‑smooth piecewise structure. You can still model it with linear inequalities and binary variables, but this is overkill for such a small problem and not usually intended here. Since the original constraints don’t link *who goes first* to *amounts*, the simple LP above is the standard and cleanest model.

---

## 5. Suitable Python libraries

To solve this LP, any LP solver interface in Python is appropriate:

- **PuLP**: Very simple for educational LPs and integrates easily with CBC solver.
- **SciPy**: `scipy.optimize.linprog` can solve this easily.
- **OR-Tools**: `ortools.linear_solver.pywraplp` (for linear programming / mixed‑integer linear programming).
- **Pyomo**: More general modelling framework if you want extensibility or nonlinear/integer extensions.

For this specific problem, **PuLP** or **SciPy’s linprog** are the most straightforward.

## 6. Example: PuLP formulation (code sketch)

```python
import pulp as pl

# Problem
prob = pl.LpProblem("Shampoo_Mixture", pl.LpMinimize)

# Variables
S = pl.LpVariable('S', lowBound=0)
G = pl.LpVariable('G', lowBound=0)

# Objective: minimize total effectiveness time
prob += 0.5 * S + 0.75 * G

# Constraints
prob += S + G == 400          # total units
prob += S >= 100              # min sulfate
prob += S <= 2 * G            # sulfate <= 2 * ginger

# Solve
prob.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

print("Status:", pl.LpStatus[prob.status])
print("Optimal S (sulfate):", pl.value(S))
print("Optimal G (ginger):", pl.value(G))
print("Minimum total time:", pl.value(prob.objective))
```

This code directly implements the formal model above.

import pulp as pl

def main():
    # Define the problem
    prob = pl.LpProblem("Shampoo_Mixture", pl.LpMinimize)

    # Decision variables
    S = pl.LpVariable('S', lowBound=0)  # Sulfate units
    G = pl.LpVariable('G', lowBound=0)  # Ginger units

    # Objective function: minimize total effectiveness time
    prob += 0.5 * S + 0.75 * G

    # Constraints
    prob += S + G == 400        # Total quantity requirement
    prob += S >= 100            # Minimum sulfate
    prob += S <= 2 * G          # Sulfate not more than twice ginger

    # Solve the problem
    prob.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

    # Get objective value
    objective_value = pl.value(prob.objective)

    # Print final objective in required format
    print(f"FINAL_OBJ: {objective_value}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
