[PLANNER]:
1. Key optimisation goal  
   - Goal: Maximize total annual profit from producing hams and pork ribs.

2. Suggested model type  
   - Model: Linear Programming (LP) with two continuous decision variables (batches of hams and pork ribs).  
   - Assumption: It is acceptable to treat “batches” as continuous (if needed, model can later be converted to integer if the context requires whole batches only).

3. Main decision variables  
   Define:
   - Variable 1: Number of batches of hams produced per year.  
   - Variable 2: Number of batches of pork ribs produced per year.

4. Objective function structure  
   - Each batch of hams generates $150 profit.  
   - Each batch of pork ribs generates $300 profit.  
   - Objective: Maximize total profit = (profit per batch of hams × batches of hams) + (profit per batch of pork ribs × batches of pork ribs).

5. Key constraints to consider  

   a) Meat slicer capacity constraint  
   - Time per batch on slicer:
     - Hams: 4 hours per batch.
     - Pork ribs: 2 hours per batch.
   - Total available slicer time: at most 4000 hours per year.  
   - Constraint: Total slicer hours used by both products ≤ 4000.

   b) Meat packer capacity constraint  
   - Time per batch on packer:
     - Hams: 2.5 hours per batch.
     - Pork ribs: 3.5 hours per batch.
   - Total available packer time: at most 4000 hours per year.  
   - Constraint: Total packer hours used by both products ≤ 4000.

   c) Non-negativity constraints  
   - Number of batches of hams ≥ 0.  
   - Number of batches of pork ribs ≥ 0.

6. Modelling/solution strategy  

   - Step 1: Translate the above into a standard LP with two decision variables and linear constraints.
   - Step 2: Use a graphical method or a standard LP solver:
     - Plot or compute the feasible region defined by slicer and packer constraints and non-negativity.
     - Identify intersection points (corner points) of the feasible region.
   - Step 3: Evaluate the objective function at all corner points to find the maximum profit.
   - Step 4: Report:
     - Optimal numbers of batches of hams and pork ribs.
     - The corresponding maximum annual profit.
     - Utilization of each machine at the optimum (optional but useful check: whether one or both machines are fully utilized).

## 1. Decision variables

Let

- \(x\) = number of batches of hams produced per year  
- \(y\) = number of batches of pork ribs produced per year  

(We model them as continuous; they can later be required to be integers if needed.)

---

## 2. Objective function

Profit per batch:
- Hams: \$150 per batch  
- Pork ribs: \$300 per batch  

Maximize total annual profit:

\[
\max \; Z = 150x + 300y
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

### a) Meat slicer capacity

Time required per batch:
- Hams: 4 hours/batch  
- Pork ribs: 2 hours/batch  

Total slicer time available: at most 4000 hours/year:

\[
4x + 2y \le 4000
\]

---

### b) Meat packer capacity

Time required per batch:
- Hams: 2.5 hours/batch  
- Pork ribs: 3.5 hours/batch  

Total packer time available: at most 4000 hours/year:

\[
2.5x + 3.5y \le 4000
\]

---

### c) Non-negativity

\[
x \ge 0, \quad y \ge 0
\]

---

## 4. Complete LP model (standard form)

\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{Maximize} \quad & Z = 150x + 300y \\
\text{subject to} \quad
& 4x + 2y \le 4000 \quad &\text{(slicer capacity)} \\
& 2.5x + 3.5y \le 4000 \quad &\text{(packer capacity)} \\
& x \ge 0, \; y \ge 0 \quad &\text{(non-negativity)}
\end{aligned}
\]

## 5. Suitable Python libraries

Any standard LP solver can handle this small model. Common choices:

- **PuLP**  
  - Easy to model LPs with a clear algebraic style.
  - Can use CBC (default) or other installed solvers.
  - Good for teaching and quick prototypes.

- **SciPy (`scipy.optimize.linprog`)**  
  - Efficient LP solver, especially for continuous LPs.
  - Interface is more matrix/vector based (less “algebraic” but very standard).

- **OR-Tools (Google OR-Tools, `ortools.linear_solver`)**  
  - Industrial-strength; supports LP, MIP, CP.
  - Good if you later extend to integer programming or more complex models.

For this problem, **PuLP** or **SciPy’s `linprog`** would be the most straightforward.

## 6. Example of how you’d set this up in PuLP

```python
import pulp as pl

# Create the problem
prob = pl.LpProblem("Meat_Production", pl.LpMaximize)

# Decision variables
x = pl.LpVariable("Hams", lowBound=0)
y = pl.LpVariable("Pork_Ribs", lowBound=0)

# Objective
prob += 150 * x + 300 * y, "Total_Profit"

# Constraints
prob += 4 * x + 2 * y <= 4000, "Slicer_Capacity"
prob += 2.5 * x + 3.5 * y <= 4000, "Packer_Capacity"

# Solve
prob.solve()

# Results
print("Status:", pl.LpStatus[prob.status])
print("Optimal hams (batches):", x.varValue)
print("Optimal pork ribs (batches):", y.varValue)
print("Max profit:", pl.value(prob.objective))
```
