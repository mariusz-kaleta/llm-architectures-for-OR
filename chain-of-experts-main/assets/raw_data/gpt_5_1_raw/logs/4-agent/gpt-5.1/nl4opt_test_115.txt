1. **Key optimisation goal**

   - **Goal:** Minimize the total daily production cost of running the factories while meeting minimum teddy bear demand.

2. **Suggested model type**

   - **Model:** Linear Programming (LP), deterministic, single-period (one day), cost-minimisation.

3. **Main decision variables**

   Define variables as hours of operation for each factory per day:

   - \(x_1\): Number of hours per day to run **Factory 1**  
   - \(x_2\): Number of hours per day to run **Factory 2**

   These will be continuous, non-negative variables (can run a fraction of an hour unless explicitly restricted to integers).

4. **Objective function components**

   - Cost of running Factory 1: 300 dollars per hour → total cost: \(300 x_1\)
   - Cost of running Factory 2: 600 dollars per hour → total cost: \(600 x_2\)

   The objective function is the sum of these:

   - **Minimize** total cost = \(300 x_1 + 600 x_2\)

5. **Key constraints to model**

   Translate production and demand into linear inequalities:

   a) **Black teddy bear demand constraint**

   - Factory 1 produces 5 black bears per hour → total black from F1: \(5 x_1\)
   - Factory 2 produces 10 black bears per hour → total black from F2: \(10 x_2\)
   - Demand requirement: at least 20 black bears per day

   → Constraint:  
   \(5 x_1 + 10 x_2 \ge 20\)

   b) **White teddy bear demand constraint**

   - Factory 1 produces 6 white bears per hour → total white from F1: \(6 x_1\)
   - Factory 2 produces 10 white bears per hour → total white from F2: \(10 x_2\)
   - Demand requirement: at least 5 white bears per day

   → Constraint:  
   \(6 x_1 + 10 x_2 \ge 5\)

   c) **Brown teddy bear demand constraint**

   - Factory 1 produces 3 brown bears per hour → total brown from F1: \(3 x_1\)
   - Factory 2 produces 0 brown bears → no contribution
   - Demand requirement: at least 15 brown bears per day

   → Constraint:  
   \(3 x_1 \ge 15\)

   d) **Non-negativity constraints**

   - Factories cannot run a negative number of hours:

   → Constraints:  
   \(x_1 \ge 0\)  
   \(x_2 \ge 0\)

6. **Optional modelling considerations (if needed by instructor/setting)**

   - If production must be in whole hours, the model would become an Integer Linear Program (ILP) with \(x_1, x_2\) integers.
   - If there is any maximum number of operating hours per day (e.g., 24 hours), add upper bounds: \(x_1 \le 24\), \(x_2 \le 24\). (Not required by the given text, but something the modeller could consider.)

## 1. Decision variables

Let

- \(x_1 \ge 0\): hours per day to run Factory 1  
- \(x_2 \ge 0\): hours per day to run Factory 2  

(Continuous variables; if hours must be whole numbers, they would be integer.)

---

## 2. Objective function

Minimize the total daily cost of running the factories.

- Cost of Factory 1: \(300\) dollars/hour → \(300 x_1\)  
- Cost of Factory 2: \(600\) dollars/hour → \(600 x_2\)

Objective:

\[
\min \; Z = 300 x_1 + 600 x_2
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

### a) Black teddy bear demand

Per hour:
- Factory 1: 5 black bears  
- Factory 2: 10 black bears  

Daily demand: at least 20 black bears.

\[
5 x_1 + 10 x_2 \;\ge\; 20
\]

---

### b) White teddy bear demand

Per hour:
- Factory 1: 6 white bears  
- Factory 2: 10 white bears  

Daily demand: at least 5 white bears.

\[
6 x_1 + 10 x_2 \;\ge\; 5
\]

---

### c) Brown teddy bear demand

Per hour:
- Factory 1: 3 brown bears  
- Factory 2: 0 brown bears  

Daily demand: at least 15 brown bears.

\[
3 x_1 \;\ge\; 15
\]

---

### d) Non-negativity

\[
x_1 \ge 0,\quad x_2 \ge 0
\]

(Optionally, you might add \(x_1 \le 24,\; x_2 \le 24\) if factories can’t run more than 24 hours per day.)

## 4. Model summary (standard LP form)

\[
\begin{aligned}
\min\quad & 300 x_1 + 600 x_2 \\
\text{s.t.}\quad
& 5 x_1 + 10 x_2 \ge 20 && \text{(black bears)} \\
& 6 x_1 + 10 x_2 \ge 5  && \text{(white bears)} \\
& 3 x_1 \ge 15          && \text{(brown bears)} \\

## 5. Suitable Python libraries

Any LP library is fine. Common choices:

- **PuLP**: simple and very good for teaching/learning LP.
- **OR-Tools (Google)**: powerful, with linear solver `pywraplp`.
- **Pyomo**: good for larger or more complex models.

For this problem, **PuLP** is a good fit.

### Example in PuLP

```python
import pulp as pl

# Create problem
prob = pl.LpProblem("TeddyBearProduction", pl.LpMinimize)

# Decision variables (continuous, >= 0)
x1 = pl.LpVariable('x1', lowBound=0)  # hours of Factory 1
x2 = pl.LpVariable('x2', lowBound=0)  # hours of Factory 2

# Objective function
prob += 300 * x1 + 600 * x2

# Constraints
prob += 5 * x1 + 10 * x2 >= 20   # black bears
prob += 6 * x1 + 10 * x2 >= 5    # white bears
prob += 3 * x1 >= 15             # brown bears

# Solve
prob.solve()

print("Status:", pl.LpStatus[prob.status])
print("Optimal hours for Factory 1 (x1):", pl.value(x1))
print("Optimal hours for Factory 2 (x2):", pl.value(x2))
print("Minimum total cost:", pl.value(prob.objective))
```

x1 = pl.LpVariable('x1', lowBound=0, cat='Integer')

import pulp as pl

def main():
    # Create the LP problem: minimization
    prob = pl.LpProblem("TeddyBearProduction", pl.LpMinimize)

    # Decision variables (continuous, non-negative)
    x1 = pl.LpVariable('x1', lowBound=0)  # hours of Factory 1
    x2 = pl.LpVariable('x2', lowBound=0)  # hours of Factory 2

    # Objective function: minimize total daily cost
    prob += 300 * x1 + 600 * x2, "Total_Cost"

    # Constraints
    # Black bears: 5 x1 + 10 x2 >= 20
    prob += 5 * x1 + 10 * x2 >= 20, "Black_Bear_Demand"

    # White bears: 6 x1 + 10 x2 >= 5
    prob += 6 * x1 + 10 * x2 >= 5, "White_Bear_Demand"

    # Brown bears: 3 x1 >= 15
    prob += 3 * x1 >= 15, "Brown_Bear_Demand"

    # Solve the problem
    prob.solve()

    # Extract the objective value
    final_obj_value = pl.value(prob.objective)

    # Print required outputs (can be extended if desired)
    # Final required line:
    print(f"FINAL_OBJ: {final_obj_value}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
