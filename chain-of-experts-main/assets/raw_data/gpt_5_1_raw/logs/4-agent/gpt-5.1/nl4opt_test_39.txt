## 1. Decision variables

Let

- \(x \ge 0\): gallons of chocolate ice cream produced per week  
- \(y \ge 0\): gallons of vanilla ice cream produced per week  

(Because of “at least 5 gallons” constraints, non-negativity is automatically satisfied, but we keep it conceptually.)

---

## 2. Objective function

Profit per gallon:

- Chocolate: \$200 per gallon  
- Vanilla: \$300 per gallon  

Total weekly profit:
\[
\text{Profit} = 200x + 300y
\]

**Objective:**
\[
\max\; 200x + 300y
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

### a) Minimum production requirements

At least 5 gallons of each:

\[
x \ge 5
\]
\[
y \ge 5
\]

---

### b) Maximum production capacities

At most 10 gallons of chocolate, 8 of vanilla:

\[
x \le 10
\]
\[
y \le 8
\]

---

### c) Time (machine/production) capacity

Production times:

- Chocolate: 1 hour per gallon  
- Vanilla: 2 hours per gallon  

Total production time available: 30 hours per week.

Time usage:
\[
1x + 2y \le 30
\]

---

### d) Worker requirement interpretation

Statement:  
“at least 6 workers are needed with 1 working on the chocolate ice cream and 2 on the vanilla ice cream at any time.”

- There is no total number of worker-hours or total workers per week specified.
- The requirement describes a *staffing pattern* (1 on chocolate, 2 on vanilla, total ≥ 6) that must be maintained at any production time, but with no given upper bound on worker-hours.

Because no numerical limit is given, it does **not** impose an extra inequality involving \(x\) and \(y\); it is an operational/descriptive condition, not a binding resource in this LP.

So in this model we **do not** add an extra worker constraint.

---

## 4. Full LP model

\[
\begin{aligned}
\max\quad  & 200x + 300y \\
\text{s.t.}\quad 
& x \ge 5 \\
& y \ge 5 \\
& x \le 10 \\
& y \le 8 \\
& x + 2y \le 30 \\
& x, y \ge 0
\end{aligned}
\]

This is a 2-variable linear program with a convex polygon feasible region.

---

## 5. Suitable Python libraries

Any standard LP solver works. Common choices:

- `PuLP` (pure Python LP modeling, uses CBC by default)
- `scipy.optimize.linprog` (SciPy’s LP solver)
- `ortools.linear_solver` (Google OR-Tools, supports LP/MIP with multiple backends)
- `cvxpy` (more general convex optimization modeling)

For this type of textbook LP, **PuLP** or **SciPy’s linprog** is very convenient.

---

## 6. Example: PuLP implementation

```python
import pulp as pl

# Create the problem: maximize profit
prob = pl.LpProblem("IceCream_Production", pl.LpMaximize)

# Decision variables
x = pl.LpVariable("chocolate_gallons", lowBound=0)  # x
y = pl.LpVariable("vanilla_gallons", lowBound=0)    # y

# Objective function: maximize 200x + 300y
prob += 200 * x + 300 * y, "Total_Profit"

# Constraints
prob += x >= 5, "Min_Chocolate"
prob += y >= 5, "Min_Vanilla"
prob += x <= 10, "Max_Chocolate"
prob += y <= 8, "Max_Vanilla"
prob += x + 2 * y <= 30, "Time_Capacity"

# Solve
prob.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

print("Status:", pl.LpStatus[prob.status])
print("Optimal chocolate (x):", x.varValue)
print("Optimal vanilla (y):", y.varValue)
print("Maximum profit:", pl.value(prob.objective))
```

This will yield the optimal number of gallons of chocolate and vanilla to maximize weekly profit under the given constraints.

import pulp as pl

def main():
    # Create the problem: maximize profit
    prob = pl.LpProblem("IceCream_Production", pl.LpMaximize)

    # Decision variables
    x = pl.LpVariable("chocolate_gallons", lowBound=0)  # x
    y = pl.LpVariable("vanilla_gallons", lowBound=0)    # y

    # Objective function: maximize 200x + 300y
    prob += 200 * x + 300 * y, "Total_Profit"

    # Constraints
    prob += x >= 5, "Min_Chocolate"
    prob += y >= 5, "Min_Vanilla"
    prob += x <= 10, "Max_Chocolate"
    prob += y <= 8, "Max_Vanilla"
    prob += x + 2 * y <= 30, "Time_Capacity"

    # Solve
    prob.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

    # Extract objective value
    final_obj = pl.value(prob.objective)

    # Required final output
    print(f"FINAL_OBJ: {final_obj}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
