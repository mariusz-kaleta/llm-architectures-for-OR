1. Key optimisation goal  
Goal: Minimize total fat intake from fish and chicken meals while meeting minimum protein, iron, and preference requirements.

2. Suggested model type  
Model: Linear Programming (LP) with two continuous decision variables (or Integer LP if meals must be whole numbers).

3. Main decision variables  
Define decision variables as:
- x₁: Number of fish meals the patient eats.
- x₂: Number of chicken meals the patient eats.

(If needed, specify whether x₁, x₂ are nonnegative reals or nonnegative integers; the structure is the same.)

4. Objective function (conceptual)  
Total fat per meal type:
- Fish meal: 7 units of fat.
- Chicken meal: 10 units of fat.

Objective (to minimize):  
- Total fat = 7·x₁ + 10·x₂.

5. Key constraints to consider  

A. Protein requirement  
- Each fish meal provides 10 units of protein.  
- Each chicken meal provides 15 units of protein.  
- The patient needs at least 130 units of protein.  

Constraint (conceptual):  
- 10·x₁ + 15·x₂ ≥ 130.

B. Iron requirement  
- Each fish meal provides 12 units of iron.  
- Each chicken meal provides 8 units of iron.  
- The patient needs at least 120 units of iron.  

Constraint (conceptual):  
- 12·x₁ + 8·x₂ ≥ 120.

C. Preference on number of meals (chicken vs fish)  
- “At least twice as many chicken meals as fish meals.”  
- This creates a proportional relationship between x₂ and x₁.  

Constraint (conceptual):  
- x₂ ≥ 2·x₁.

D. Nonnegativity and integrality  
- She cannot eat a negative number of meals.  
- Depending on modelling choice, you may enforce integrality or allow continuous values and then interpret/round.  

Constraints (conceptual):  
- x₁ ≥ 0.  
- x₂ ≥ 0.  
- Optional: x₁, x₂ integers.

6. Modelling strategy / structure  

- Use a 2-variable LP: decision vector (x₁, x₂).  
- Single linear objective (minimize fat).  
- Three main linear inequality constraints for protein, iron, and chicken-fish ratio, plus nonnegativity (and potential integrality).  
- Because the feasible region is defined by linear inequalities and the objective is linear, any optimal solution will occur at a corner (extreme) point of the feasible region—this can be exploited analytically (graphical method) or via LP solver.  

7. Checks / sensitivity aspects to keep in mind  

- After solving, verify that:
  - Protein constraint is satisfied (≥ 130).  
  - Iron constraint is satisfied (≥ 120).  
  - Chicken ≥ 2·Fish is satisfied.  
- If integrality is important, check the nearest integer point(s) around the continuous optimum and choose the one with minimum fat that still satisfies all constraints.

## 1. Decision variables

Let

- \(x_1 \ge 0\): number of fish meals  
- \(x_2 \ge 0\): number of chicken meals  

If meals must be counted in whole numbers, additionally require

- \(x_1, x_2 \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}\)

---

## 2. Objective function (minimize total fat)

Fat per meal:

- Fish: 7 units fat/meal  
- Chicken: 10 units fat/meal  

Total fat:

\[
\text{Minimize } Z = 7x_1 + 10x_2
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

### a) Protein requirement

- Fish meal: 10 units protein  
- Chicken meal: 15 units protein  
- Need at least 130 units protein:

\[
10x_1 + 15x_2 \;\ge\; 130
\]

### b) Iron requirement

- Fish meal: 12 units iron  
- Chicken meal: 8 units iron  
- Need at least 120 units iron:

\[
12x_1 + 8x_2 \;\ge\; 120
\]

### c) Preference: at least twice as many chicken meals as fish meals

“at least twice as many chicken meals as fish meals” ⇒

\[
x_2 \;\ge\; 2x_1
\]

### d) Nonnegativity (and optional integrality)

\[
x_1 \ge 0,\quad x_2 \ge 0
\]

Optionally:

\[
x_1, x_2 \in \mathbb{Z}
\]

---

## 4. Model summary

**Linear Programming (LP) formulation:**

\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{Minimize } 
& Z = 7x_1 + 10x_2 \\
\text{subject to } 
& 10x_1 + 15x_2 \ge 130 \quad \text{(protein)} \\
& 12x_1 + 8x_2 \ge 120 \quad \text{(iron)} \\
& x_2 - 2x_1 \ge 0 \quad \text{(preference)} \\
& x_1 \ge 0,\; x_2 \ge 0 \quad \text{(nonnegativity)} \\
& \text{(optionally) } x_1, x_2 \in \mathbb{Z}
\end{aligned}
\]

This is a small LP (or ILP if integrality imposed).

## 5. Suitable Python libraries

Any standard LP/ILP solver can handle this:

## 6. Example PuLP implementation (continuous or integer)

```python
import pulp as pl

# Problem: minimize total fat
prob = pl.LpProblem("Diet_Fat_Minimization", pl.LpMinimize)

# Decision variables
# Use cat=pl.LpInteger for integer meals; LpContinuous for fractional
x1 = pl.LpVariable('fish_meals', lowBound=0, cat=pl.LpInteger)
x2 = pl.LpVariable('chicken_meals', lowBound=0, cat=pl.LpInteger)

# Objective function
prob += 7 * x1 + 10 * x2, "Total_Fat"

# Constraints
prob += 10 * x1 + 15 * x2 >= 130, "ProteinRequirement"
prob += 12 * x1 + 8 * x2  >= 120, "IronRequirement"
prob += x2 - 2 * x1       >= 0,   "PreferenceChickenAtLeastTwiceFish"

# Solve
prob.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

print("Status:", pl.LpStatus[prob.status])
print("Optimal number of fish meals    =", x1.value())
print("Optimal number of chicken meals =", x2.value())
print("Minimum total fat               =", pl.value(prob.objective))
```

You can switch `cat=pl.LpInteger` to `pl.LpContinuous` if fractional meals are acceptable or you only want the continuous relaxation.

    prob += 7 * x1 + 10 * x2, "Total_Fat"
