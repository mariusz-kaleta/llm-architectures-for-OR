1. Key optimisation goal  
Goal: Maximise total weekly profit from producing hardwood and vinyl flooring, subject to demand, contract, and capacity limits.

2. Suggested model type  
Model: Linear Programming (LP) with two decision variables. The feasible region is defined by linear inequalities representing minimum demand, contract requirement, and production capacity.

3. Main decision variables  
Let:
- Variable 1: Weekly production quantity of engineered hardwood flooring (in square feet).  
- Variable 2: Weekly production quantity of vinyl planks (in square feet).

4. Objective function (conceptual description)  
- Maximise:  
  (profit per sq ft of hardwood × hardwood quantity)  
  + (profit per sq ft of vinyl × vinyl quantity)

5. Key constraints to consider  

a. Minimum demand constraints  
- Hardwood production must be at least the forecast demand for hardwood.  
- Vinyl production must be at least the forecast demand for vinyl.

b. Shipping contract constraint  
- Total flooring shipped each week (hardwood + vinyl) must be at least the contractual minimum total square footage.

c. Production capacity (upper bound) constraints  
- Hardwood production cannot exceed the weekly maximum hardwood capacity (labor-limited).  
- Vinyl production cannot exceed the weekly maximum vinyl capacity (labor-limited).

d. Non-negativity constraints  
- Both decision variables must be ≥ 0.

6. Modelling strategy / solution approach  

a. Build the LP  
- Define the two variables explicitly (H for hardwood, V for vinyl).  
- Write the objective function in terms of H and V using given per-unit profits.  
- Encode all given lower and upper bounds as linear inequalities.

b. Feasible region analysis  
- Identify all constraints graphically (if solving by hand) or through LP software.  
- Find intersection (corner) points of constraint lines because the optimal LP solution will occur at a corner of the feasible region.  
- Check which corner points satisfy all constraints.

c. Optimal solution identification  
- Evaluate the objective function at each feasible corner point.  
- Choose the H and V combination that yields the highest profit while satisfying all constraints.

d. Sensitivity / sanity checks (optional for modeller)  
- Check whether any constraints are binding at the optimum (e.g., capacity, contract total, or demand minimums).  
- Optionally explore how changes in capacity or profit per square foot might affect the recommended production plan.

## 1. Decision variables

Let

- \(H\) = square feet of engineered hardwood flooring produced per week  
- \(V\) = square feet of vinyl planks produced per week  

Units: square feet (sq ft).

Both variables are continuous and nonnegative.

---

## 2. Objective function

Profit per sq ft:

- Hardwood: \$2.50  
- Vinyl: \$3.00  

Maximise total weekly profit:

\[
\max Z = 2.5H + 3V
\]

---

## 3. Constraints

### a) Minimum demand constraints

At least the forecast demand must be produced:

\[
H \;\ge\; 20{,}000
\]
\[
V \;\ge\; 10{,}000
\]

### b) Shipping contract constraint

Total square footage shipped (assumed equal to total produced) must be at least 60,000 sq ft:

\[
H + V \;\ge\; 60{,}000
\]

### c) Production capacity (upper bounds)

Due to labor limits:

\[
H \;\le\; 50{,}000
\]
\[
V \;\le\; 30{,}000
\]

### d) Non-negativity constraints

\[
H \;\ge\; 0,\quad V \;\ge\; 0
\]

(These are redundant given the demand constraints but are standard in LP models.)

---

## 4. Complete LP formulation

\[
\begin{aligned}
\text{Maximise} \quad & Z = 2.5H + 3V \\
\text{subject to} \quad
& H \ge 20{,}000 \\
& V \ge 10{,}000 \\
& H + V \ge 60{,}000 \\
& H \le 50{,}000 \\
& V \le 30{,}000 \\
& H, V \ge 0
\end{aligned}
\]

---

## 5. Solving and interpreting the optimum (conceptual)

Because vinyl has higher profit per sq ft than hardwood (\$3 vs \$2.5), the model will try to:

1. Satisfy lower bounds (demand, contract), and  
2. Use as much vinyl as possible (up to its capacity), then  
3. Use hardwood as needed to hit the contract requirement.

Key corner points defined by intersecting constraints are, for example:

- \(V = 30{,}000\) (max vinyl), then to satisfy \(H + V \ge 60{,}000\):
  \[
  H \ge 60{,}000 - 30{,}000 = 30{,}000
  \]
  This also respects \(H \ge 20{,}000\) and \(H \le 50{,}000\).  
  So one candidate is \((H,V) = (30{,}000,\;30{,}000)\).

Profit at this point:
\[
Z = 2.5(30{,}000) + 3(30{,}000) = 75{,}000 + 90{,}000 = 165{,}000
\]

Other feasible corners (e.g., hitting minimum demands and/or contract with lower vinyl) will yield lower profit because they shift production from the more profitable vinyl to less profitable hardwood, while still satisfying constraints.

Thus, the profit-maximising plan is:

- \(H^* = 30{,}000\) sq ft hardwood per week  
- \(V^* = 30{,}000\) sq ft vinyl per week  
- Maximum profit \(Z^* = \$165{,}000\) per week

Binding constraints at the optimum:

- \(H + V = 60{,}000\) (contract)
- \(V = 30{,}000\) (vinyl capacity)

Non-binding (slack present):

- \(H \ge 20{,}000\) (we produce more: 30,000)
- \(V \ge 10{,}000\) (we produce more: 30,000)
- \(H \le 50{,}000\) (we are below max: 30,000)

## 6. Suitable Python tools and skeleton code

Any LP solver will work well. Common, accessible choices:

- `pulp` (very simple and pure Python)  
- `ortools.linear_solver` (Google OR-Tools, powerful and fast)  
- `scipy.optimize.linprog` (general-purpose scientific stack)

### Example with PuLP

```python
import pulp as pl

# 1. Define the problem
model = pl.LpProblem("Flooring_Production", pl.LpMaximize)

# 2. Decision variables
H = pl.LpVariable("Hardwood", lowBound=0)
V = pl.LpVariable("Vinyl", lowBound=0)

# 3. Objective
model += 2.5 * H + 3.0 * V, "Total_Weekly_Profit"

# 4. Constraints
model += H >= 20000, "Min_Hardwood_Demand"
model += V >= 10000, "Min_Vinyl_Demand"
model += H + V >= 60000, "Min_Contract_Shipment"
model += H <= 50000, "Max_Hardwood_Capacity"
model += V <= 30000, "Max_Vinyl_Capacity"

# 5. Solve
model.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

# 6. Results
H_opt = pl.value(H)
V_opt = pl.value(V)
Z_opt = pl.value(model.objective)

print(f"Optimal hardwood (sq ft): {H_opt:.0f}")
print(f"Optimal vinyl (sq ft): {V_opt:.0f}")
print(f"Max weekly profit: ${Z_opt:,.2f}")
```

This will return approximately:

- Hardwood: 30000  
- Vinyl: 30000  
- Max weekly profit: \$165,000.00

import pulp as pl

def main():
    # Define the problem
    model = pl.LpProblem("Flooring_Production", pl.LpMaximize)

    # Decision variables
    H = pl.LpVariable("Hardwood", lowBound=0)
    V = pl.LpVariable("Vinyl", lowBound=0)

    # Objective function: Maximise profit
    model += 2.5 * H + 3.0 * V

    # Constraints
    model += H >= 20000          # Minimum hardwood demand
    model += V >= 10000          # Minimum vinyl demand
    model += H + V >= 60000      # Shipping contract
    model += H <= 50000          # Hardwood capacity
    model += V <= 30000          # Vinyl capacity

    # Solve
    model.solve(pl.PULP_CBC_CMD(msg=False))

    # Extract objective value
    Z_opt = pl.value(model.objective)

    # Final required print
    print(f"FINAL_OBJ: {Z_opt}")

if __name__ == "__main__":
    main()
